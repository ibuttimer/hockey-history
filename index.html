Overview

The FIH estimate there are around 30 million players worldwide. There are 137 National Associations of hockey, spread over five continents. When played at the highest level, hockey is an incredibly fast and skillfull game. It is an intense, physically demanding game where rolling substitutions take place throughout each game.

However, it is a very accessible game which does not required expensive kit. While most games today are played on artificial turf, hockey can be played on any surface from hard courts indoors to grass and even dirt. It is a non-contact sport played by people of all ages, from children to octagenarians. According to Guinness World Records , the oldest female player is Anne Graves who played a full match aged 80 years and 9 days.

There are a number of variations of the game, apart from the traditional eleven players outdoor format. Indoor Hockey is played by two teams of six players, using a small sized pitch which has boards along each side to keep the ball in play. The game is commonly played in multi-sports halls or other similar venues. ParaHockey (Hockey ID) is the adapted format of hockey for players with an intellectual disability, which is played with seven players per team. And Hockey 5s is a rapidly growing small-format version of the game, played by two teams of five players, which is designed to be adaptable to multiple surfaces. Whereas, Beach Hockey is a complementary version of the sport, adapted to be played on sand, with five players per team.

Origins

The game of hockey as we know it today, has its roots in the distant past. Historical records reveal that the Egyptians played a rudimentary form of the game 4,000 years ago.
Also a form of the game was played in Iran around 2,000 BC, and in Ethiopia around 1,000 BC. The Arabs, Greeks, Persians, and Romans each had their own versions,and traces of a stick game played by the Aztec Indians of South America have been found.   It is believed that the game gets it name from the French word 'hocquet', meaning shepherd's crook in reference to the shape of the players' stick.
In the mid-18th century, the growth of public schools, such as Eton, lead to the modern game emerging. In 1876, the first Hockey Association was formed in the UK, and drew up the first formal set of rules. This association only lasted for six years, but in 1886 the Hockey Association was revived in London by nine founding member clubs.
